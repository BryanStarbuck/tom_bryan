# Washington State & King County

What is unique about doing this search in Washington — and specifically in King
County, where Redmond, Bellevue, and Kirkland sit? Several things shape how Tom's
search should run.

## 1. Washington was first to regulate senior referral agencies

Washington is the **only-state-to-go-first**: the **Elder and Vulnerable Adult
Referral Agency Act of 2011 (RCW 18.330)** made it the first state in the nation to
regulate senior-housing referral agencies. Practically, this means brokers Tom
works with here are **legally required to give written fee disclosure** before
referrals — a consumer protection that does not exist in many other states. See
[Regulations](./04-regulations.md). Use it: ask for that disclosure.

## 2. A mature, well-developed advisor and adult-family-home market

The Puget Sound region has an unusually deep bench of senior placement advisors
(many in our `../brokers.csv`) and a large, well-established network of licensed
**Adult Family Homes** — small residential care homes. Compared with many regions,
families here have more advisor options and more small-home alternatives to large
facilities.

## 3. High cost and tight supply on the Eastside

Redmond, Bellevue, and Kirkland are among the most **expensive** housing markets in
the state (tech-driven East King County). For Tom this means:

* Market-rate senior and regular apartments run high; budget will heavily shape
  options.
* Demand is strong and good units move quickly — an advisor's real-time
  availability knowledge is more valuable here.

## 4. Subsidized senior housing is scarce and waitlisted

If Tom needs **income-based** housing, King County is hard:

* Subsidized and **HUD Section 202** senior buildings have waits of **months to
  years**.
* Some public-housing and Section 8 waitlists are **periodically closed** to new
  applicants (both were closed as of mid-2026), though certain senior-specific
  programs open at times.
* Eligibility for HUD 202: household income under ~**50% of Area Median Income**,
  at least one member **62+**. King County's AMI is high, which can change who
  qualifies.
* **Strategy:** apply to **many** waitlists as early as possible. Local resources:
  **King County Housing Authority (KCHA)**, **Seattle Housing Authority (SHA)** /
  Seattle Senior Housing Program, and **Sound Generations** (publishes a list of
  subsidized/low-income senior housing).

## 5. Strong tenant and senior protections

Washington has comparatively strong renter protections — the **Residential
Landlord-Tenant Act (RCW 59.18)**, the **Washington Law Against Discrimination
(RCW 49.60)** with disability/reasonable-accommodation requirements, and an active
**Long-Term Care Ombudsman** for licensed care settings. Tom has real rights as a
renter and as a care-community resident.

## Putting it together for Tom's search

* **Lean on the WA fee-disclosure rule** to vet brokers (ask for it in writing).
* **For market-rate** senior/independent living: use 2–3 Eastside advisors plus
  direct contact; expect Eastside prices.
* **For subsidized** housing: skip brokers, get on KCHA / SHA / building-specific
  **waitlists now** — the wait is the real obstacle, not access.
* **If selling a home** to fund the move: add an **SRES** real-estate agent (e.g.,
  Brenner | Hill in `../brokers.csv`).
