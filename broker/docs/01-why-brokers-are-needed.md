# Why People Seem to Need Brokers

When families start looking for senior housing, they quickly run into a confusing,
fragmented market. A **senior living placement advisor** (often called a senior
care advisor, placement agent, or senior housing broker) exists to cut through
that. Here is why people lean on them.

## The market is large, scattered, and hard to compare

In the greater Seattle / Eastside area there are dozens of senior communities of
very different types — independent living, assisted living, memory care, adult
family homes, and subsidized senior apartments — each with different pricing,
care levels, availability, and quality. There is **no single, reliable public
catalog** that shows real-time openings, real prices, and honest quality notes.
Families researching alone spend weeks calling communities one at a time.

A good advisor already holds that map. The better local firms say they
**personally visit and vet** the communities, keep insider notes on culture and
care quality, and track who actually has openings now.

## What a placement advisor actually does

* Does an intake assessment — budget, care needs, location, timeline.
* Narrows dozens of options to a short list that fits.
* Knows current availability and rough pricing (which is often not published).
* Arranges and sometimes accompanies tours.
* Helps with the paperwork and the move-in logistics.
* Can flag care-level mismatches a family might not catch (e.g., a community that
  cannot handle a medical need).

The strongest local examples are RN-led firms (advisors who are registered nurses
with clinical experience), which matters when the senior has real medical needs.

## Why families choose to use one

* **It is usually free to the family.** The community pays the advisor a referral
  fee, so there is no direct cost to most families (see
  [Why Apartments Use Brokers](./05-why-apartments-use-brokers.md)).
* **Speed.** When a move is urgent (a hospital discharge, a fall, a caregiver who
  can no longer cope), an advisor can produce vetted options in days.
* **Local knowledge.** They know which communities are good, which have openings,
  and which to avoid — information that is hard to get from the outside.
* **One point of contact** instead of cold-calling 20 places.

## The trade-off to keep in mind

Because the advisor is paid by the community, there is a **built-in conflict of
interest**: an agency only earns a fee from communities it has a contract with, so
its short list may be limited to those partners. The practical defenses:

* Ask which communities they are contracted with, and whether they will also show
  you options they are **not** paid by.
* Ask how their fee is calculated (Washington law requires them to disclose this
  in writing — see [Regulations](./04-regulations.md)).
* Use more than one advisor, and do some independent looking too.

## How this applies to Tom

For Tom's search in Redmond / Bellevue / Kirkland, an advisor is most valuable if
Tom wants a **senior community** (independent or assisted living). If Tom mainly
wants an ordinary rental apartment, a placement advisor is less of a fit — confirm
each candidate also handles independent-living / rental-style placement, not only
assisted living. See [Senior Apartment Types](./03-senior-apartment-types.md).
