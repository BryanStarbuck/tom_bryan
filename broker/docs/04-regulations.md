# Regulations

Are there laws about senior-housing brokers and the buildings they refer to? Yes —
and Washington is one of the most regulated states in the country for this. This
matters because it gives Tom concrete rights and gives us tools to vet a broker.

## Washington regulates senior referral agencies — first in the nation

Washington passed the **Elder and Vulnerable Adult Referral Agency Act of 2011**,
codified at **[RCW 18.330](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=18.330&full=true)**.
Washington was the **first state in the U.S.** to regulate senior-housing referral
agencies. The act sets minimum standards of conduct and transparency for the
placement / referral industry.

### Mandatory written fee disclosure (RCW 18.330.050)
Before making a referral, an agency **must give the client a written disclosure
statement, and the client must acknowledge it**. The agency keeps a copy. The
disclosure must state:

* The agency's name, address, and phone number.
* The amount of any fee the agency receives **from the client** (if any).
* If the fee is instead paid **by the provider/community**, the method of
  computing the fee and the timing/method of payment.

In short: a Washington broker is **legally required to tell you, in writing and up
front, how and by whom they get paid.** Always get this before accepting referrals.

### Record-keeping (RCW 18.330.040)
Agencies must keep referral records including the client's name, address, phone,
the services sought, the location and likely duration of services, the monthly or
unit cost, the agency's fee if any, and any refund dates and amounts.

## Fair housing protects seniors as renters

Beyond referral-agency rules, ordinary housing law protects Tom as a renter:

* **Federal Fair Housing Act** and **Washington Law Against Discrimination
  (RCW 49.60)** bar discrimination, including on the basis of disability — which
  covers many seniors — and require **reasonable accommodations**.
* **Age-restricted (55+/62+) communities** are a recognized, lawful exception to
  the "familial status" rule (the federal Housing for Older Persons Act), which is
  why they can legally limit residency by age.
* Washington's **Residential Landlord-Tenant Act (RCW 59.18)** governs normal
  apartment rentals — deposits, notice, habitability, eviction protections.

## Licensing of the housing itself

The communities (not the brokers) are separately licensed/regulated:

* **Assisted Living Facilities** — [RCW 18.20](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=18.20),
  overseen by the WA Dept. of Social and Health Services (DSHS).
* **Adult Family Homes** and **nursing homes** have their own licensing chapters.
* The **Washington State Long-Term Care Ombudsman** advocates for residents of
  these licensed settings and can be a free resource if problems arise.

## What this means for Tom — a practical checklist

* Ask every broker for their **RCW 18.330 written fee-disclosure statement** before
  accepting any referral. A reputable WA advisor will provide it without being
  asked.
* Confirm whether **you** pay anything (most are free to families) and how the
  community-paid fee is computed.
* For any care community, you can check **licensing and inspection history** with
  DSHS.
* Know the **anti-discrimination and reasonable-accommodation** rights if a
  disability is involved.
