# Why Apartments Use Brokers

Are there reasons the apartments/communities themselves want brokers in the
picture? Yes. Understanding the business model explains why the service is "free"
to families and why communities steer you toward advisors.

## The referral-fee model in one paragraph

Senior communities treat placement advisors as an **outsourced sales channel**.
When an advisor refers a family and that person moves in, the **community pays the
advisor a referral fee** — commonly around **70–80% of the first month's rent**,
often roughly **$3,000–$5,000 per placement**. The family pays the advisor
**nothing**. The advisor's commission comes entirely from the community.

## Why communities are happy to pay it

* **Pay-for-performance marketing.** The community only pays when a unit actually
  fills. That is cheaper and lower-risk than running their own ad campaigns to
  fill vacancies.
* **Pre-qualified, ready-to-move families.** Advisors screen for budget, care
  needs, and timing, so the community gets serious prospects, not tire-kickers.
* **Filling vacancies fast.** An empty senior unit is lost revenue every day.
  Advisors have a pipeline of families who need to move soon.
* **Outsourced labor.** Tours, paperwork help, and follow-up are handled by the
  advisor, freeing the community's staff.

## Why this is good — and where to be careful

**Good for families:** expert help at no direct cost, which is why so many people
use advisors.

**The catch:** because the community pays, the advisor has a financial incentive to
place you somewhere they have a **contract** with. That can narrow your options to
their partner communities and, in the worst case, nudge you toward a place that
pays well rather than the best fit. This conflict of interest is exactly why
Washington requires written fee disclosure (see [Regulations](./04-regulations.md))
and why national elder-care referral services have drawn scrutiny.

### How to protect Tom

* Ask: **"Which communities are you contracted with, and will you also show me
  ones you're not paid by?"**
* Get the **written fee disclosure** (required under RCW 18.330) before referrals.
* Use **two or three advisors** so their partner lists overlap and you see more of
  the market.
* Cross-check any recommended community independently (reviews, DSHS licensing).

## Important contrast: ordinary and subsidized apartments

This referral-fee model is specific to **senior care / community living**. It does
**not** apply to:

* **Regular market-rate apartments** — landlords usually do not pay senior
  placement advisors; you apply directly.
* **Subsidized / income-based senior housing** — these are filled by **waitlist**,
  not by paid referrals. No broker fee, and no broker can move you up the list.

So if Tom's target is a plain apartment or subsidized senior housing, the
"free broker" model mostly does not apply — go direct.
