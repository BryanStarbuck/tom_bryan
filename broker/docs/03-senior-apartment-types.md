# Senior Apartment Types

We care about finding a **senior apartment** for Tom. But "senior apartment" can
mean several very different things, and which one applies changes whether a broker
even helps and what the costs and rules are. This file lays out the options so we
can match Tom to the right category.

## The main categories

### 1. 55+ / Active Adult Apartments (age-restricted rentals)
* Ordinary market-rate apartments restricted to residents 55+ (or 62+).
* **No care services** — just an age-qualified community, often with amenities.
* Rent like a normal apartment; you simply apply.
* A placement advisor is **not really needed**; a regular apartment search or an
  age-restricted-community search works. An SRES real-estate agent can help if
  Tom is also **selling a home** to fund the move.

### 2. Independent Living (senior communities)
* Apartment-style living for seniors who are largely self-sufficient.
* Bundles meals, housekeeping, transportation, and social activities.
* Priced higher than a bare apartment because of the services.
* Placement advisors **do** cover these and can be useful, since pricing and
  availability are not public.

### 3. Assisted Living
* For seniors who need daily help (medication, bathing, dressing, mobility).
* Apartment or studio plus a care package; priced by care level.
* This is the **core market** for senior placement advisors. RN-led advisors are
  especially valuable here for matching medical needs.

### 4. Memory Care
* Secured settings for dementia/Alzheimer's. Specialized and higher cost.
* Squarely in the placement-advisor wheelhouse.

### 5. Adult Family Homes (AFH)
* Small residential homes (typically up to 6 residents) licensed for care.
* Common and well-developed in Washington; advisors place into these frequently.

### 6. Subsidized / Income-Based Senior Apartments
* Government-supported affordable housing for low-income seniors.
* Includes **HUD Section 202** (very low-income, age 62+, independent living, some
  support services) and local housing-authority senior programs.
* Eligibility: typically household income under ~50% of Area Median Income; at
  least one member 62+.
* **Brokers/advisors do not place here** and cannot skip the line — access is via
  **waitlists**, which in King County can run months to years and are sometimes
  closed. Apply to many, early.

### 7. Continuing Care Retirement Communities (CCRC / Life Plan)
* Campuses offering independent → assisted → skilled nursing on one site, so a
  resident can age in place. Often a large entry fee plus monthly fees.

## Which type fits Tom? (to confirm)

The right path depends on three questions:

1. **Care needs** — fully independent, or needs daily help? (Independent/55+ vs.
   assisted living / memory care / AFH.)
2. **Budget** — market-rate, or does he need income-based subsidized housing?
3. **Selling a home?** — if yes, an SRES real-estate agent (e.g., Brenner | Hill
   in `../brokers.csv`) handles the sale/downsizing side.

> Note for the broker list: most candidates in `../brokers.csv` specialize in
> **assisted living / care placement**. If Tom wants a plain independent or 55+
> **apartment**, confirm the advisor handles that, or pursue a direct
> apartment / age-restricted-community search instead.
