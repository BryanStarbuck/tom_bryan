## Why this is (or isn't) a fit

The fit problem is price, and it is not close. But Airbnb has genuine advantages that nothing else in this search matches, and they are worth stating plainly before dismissing it:

* **No credit check and no application.** For a retiree, this removes the single biggest structural obstacle in this whole search — the 2.5–3× income requirement that most apartment buildings impose.
* **Fully furnished and turnkey.** No furniture rental, no delivery, no utility accounts, no second move.
* **Book today, move in this week.** Nothing else here is that fast.
* **Cancellable.** Most listings let you back out if the long-term lease comes together early.

For a 4–8 week bridge stay, Airbnb is the lowest-friction path that exists. It is just expensive.

The privacy problem is real and specific: the cheap Eastside listings — the ones that could actually clear budget — are disproportionately basement ADUs and mother-in-law units attached to an owner-occupied house. If you filter by price alone and don't set **Type of Place → Entire place**, Tom ends up sharing a house with the owner. That violates the charter's no-roommates rule. The filter is not optional.

## The price math

**The median case (this is what you should expect):**

Bellevue entire-home **median** nightly rate is **$194**. (For context, the average daily rate across all listings runs higher — Bellevue $259, Redmond $230, Kirkland $273 with a $208 median — because a few expensive listings pull the average up. The median is the honest number.)

| Line item | 30 nights |
|---|---|
| $194 × 30 nights | $5,820 |
| Monthly discount at 25% | −$1,455 → **$4,365** |
| Cleaning fee | +$100 → $4,465 |
| Guest service fee (~14%) | +$625 → **$5,090** |
| WA lodging + sales tax | **$0** — see below |
| **Real 30-day total** | **≈ $5,090** |

That is **over 50% above the $3,300 budget**. At 60 nights you are looking at roughly **$10,000**, though a longer stay may pull a slightly better host discount.

**About that monthly discount — don't count on it.** Hosts set it themselves anywhere from 0% to 50%, the **average is 19%**, and **only about 32% of hosts offer one at all**. The 25% used above is already optimistic. Two-thirds of listings give you nothing.

**The only path under $3,300 — the bottom quartile:**

Bellevue's bottom-25% listings run **$117/night**; Kirkland's run **$129**. Worked at the Kirkland number:

| Line item | 30 nights |
|---|---|
| $129 × 30 nights | $3,870 |
| Monthly discount at 30% | −$1,161 → $2,709 |
| Cleaning fee | +$100 → $2,809 |
| Guest service fee (~14%) | +$393 → **$3,202** |
| WA lodging + sales tax | **$0** |
| **All-in total** | **≈ $3,202** |

That clears budget — by about $100. But look at what it required: a bottom-quartile nightly rate **and** a host offering a 30% monthly discount, when only a third of hosts offer any discount and the average is 19%. Both conditions have to hold at once, in an entire-place unit, on Tom's dates.

**It's a needle, not a market.** It exists. You just can't plan around it.

*All service fee figures are estimates: Airbnb's guest service fee runs 14.1–16.5% of the subtotal and varies by booking. Cleaning fees vary by listing; ~$100 is typical for an Eastside studio/1BR.*

## How to book

**Book 30 nights. Never 28.** This is the single most actionable tip on this page.

Under [WAC 458-20-166](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=458-20-166), a guest becomes "nontransient" at **30 continuous days**, and Washington lodging + sales tax stops applying. On these platforms that is worth roughly **10–13%** — several hundred dollars.

Airbnb's monthly filter defaults to a **28-night minimum**. That is exactly two nights short of the exemption. A 28-night booking pays full tax; a 30-night booking pays none. Two extra nights of nightly rate buy back far more than they cost.

**Search setup:**

1. Go to https://www.airbnb.com/redmond-wa/stays/monthly
2. Set the stay to **30 nights minimum** — override the 28-night default
3. **Type of Place → Entire place.** Not "Private room." This is the roommate filter and it is mandatory.
4. Sort by price and work the bottom quartile ($117–$129/night)
5. Check each candidate's **monthly discount** before getting attached — two-thirds of hosts offer none
6. Read the listing for whether it is a basement ADU under an occupied house, even if it says "entire place"

Expect to be disappointed by the price. Keep it as the fast fallback if the phone calls to the extended-stay hotels come up empty.
