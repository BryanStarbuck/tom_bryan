## Why this is (or isn't) a fit

Vrbo does one thing better than Airbnb here: **privacy is not a risk**. Vrbo is a whole-property platform, so you won't accidentally book a room in someone's occupied house the way you can on Airbnb. The no-roommates rule takes care of itself.

That's the end of the good news.

**Problem one — the discount is half as good.** Vrbo's own monthly-discount page advertises an average saving of just **10%**. Airbnb's average is 19%. That gap is the whole ballgame on a 30-night stay, and it's Vrbo's own published number, not an outside estimate.

**Problem two — and this is the structural one — Vrbo has the wrong inventory.** Vrbo's Eastside supply skews to whole houses and larger vacation properties. The studio / 1BR / ADU segment is what produces Airbnb's rare sub-$3,300 outliers, and on Vrbo's Eastside that segment **barely exists**. So the one trick that makes Airbnb theoretically viable — dig into the bottom quartile of small units — has almost nothing to dig into here.

Put together: Vrbo asks you to pay for more house than Tom needs, and gives you a weaker discount on it. That is the wrong direction on both axes.

## The price math

**The median case, at the Eastside median of $200/night:**

| Line item | 30 nights |
|---|---|
| $200 × 30 nights | $6,000 |
| Monthly discount at 10% *(Vrbo's own advertised average)* | −$600 → $5,400 |
| Cleaning fee | +$100 → $5,500 |
| Service fee (~10% on a 30-night stay) | +$550 → **$6,050** |
| WA lodging + sales tax | **$0** — see below |
| **Real 30-day total** | **≈ $6,050** |

Roughly **$12,100 for 60 nights**. That is 83% over budget.

**Even at the bottom quartile it still fails.** Take the $129/night bottom-quartile figure that just barely works on Airbnb:

| Line item | 30 nights |
|---|---|
| $129 × 30 nights | $3,870 |
| Monthly discount at 10% | −$387 → $3,483 |
| Cleaning fee | +$100 → $3,583 |
| Service fee (~10%) | +$358 → **$3,940** |
| **All-in total** | **≈ $3,940 — still over budget** |

This is the clearest way to see the problem. The **same nightly rate** that lands at $3,202 on Airbnb lands at $3,940 on Vrbo. Nothing changed but the discount, and the weak 10% simply can't do the work. And that's before accounting for the fact that $129/night Eastside small units barely exist on Vrbo in the first place.

**Note on the service fee — this is an estimate.** Vrbo's guest service fee is a sliding percentage of the reservation total that *shrinks as booking value rises*: roughly 15% on small bookings, dropping to around 10% on a 30-night stay. **Vrbo does not publish the curve**, so the ~10% used above is an informed estimate, not a quoted number. It is the one platform mechanic that works in your favor on a long stay — it just isn't nearly enough to offset the discount gap.

## How to book

**Book 30 nights. Never 28.** Same rule as Airbnb, same reason.

Under [WAC 458-20-166](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=458-20-166), a guest becomes "nontransient" at **30 continuous days** and Washington lodging + sales tax stops applying — worth roughly **10–13%** on these platforms. Vrbo's monthly filter, like Airbnb's, defaults to a **28-night minimum**, which is exactly two nights short of the exemption. Two extra nights of rate buy back far more than they cost.

If you search it anyway:

1. https://www.vrbo.com/en-ca/travel/monthly-discount
2. Set **30 nights minimum**, overriding the 28-night default
3. Filter to the smallest units available — accept that the list will be short
4. Check the monthly discount on each listing; 10% average means many offer less

Realistically: check Vrbo only after Airbnb and the extended-stay hotels have both come up empty. It is a backstop, not a plan.
