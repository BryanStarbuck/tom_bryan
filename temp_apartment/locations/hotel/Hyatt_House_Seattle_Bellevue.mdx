## Why this is (or isn't) a fit

The published number breaks the budget: **$3,630–$5,490/month**, which is $330 to $2,190 over
the $3,300 ceiling. On that number alone you would skip it.

**Don't skip it.** Hyatt House is a purpose-built extended-stay brand, and it operates a
**formal extended-stay program that is never visible online**. The rack rate you can see has
essentially no relationship to what a sales manager will quote for 30 or 60 unbroken nights.
This is the same dynamic as [Residence Inn Seattle Bellevue](./Residence_Inn_Seattle_Bellevue.mdx)
and [Hyatt House Redmond](./Hyatt_House_Seattle_Redmond.mdx) — the price on the website is the
price for a tourist, and Tom is not a tourist.

The product is good. Studio kitchen suite with a real kitchen (**no oven** — confirm what that
means for the stovetop), free hot breakfast, **free self-parking including covered spaces**, and
**~12 minutes to Redmond**, which is better than [Larkspur](./Larkspur_Landing_Bellevue.mdx) at
20 and [ESA Factoria](./Extended_Stay_America_Bellevue_Factoria.mdx) at 18. Free covered parking
is worth flagging — it is the thing that quietly breaks
[ESA Bellevue Downtown](./Extended_Stay_America_Bellevue_Downtown.mdx).

Eastgate is East Bellevue, which is the second location preference. Privacy is complete.

The honest position: **this is a second-tier call, made after the properties that are already at
or under budget.** If Larkspur reconfirms at $2,550 and ESA Overlake quotes near $2,000, there
is no reason to negotiate a $3,630 rate down. But if those come back full or higher than
expected, Hyatt House is exactly the kind of property where a phone call can move the number a
long way.

Senior-friendliness is "Partial" — no elevator or grab-bar detail is documented.

## The price math

**Price confidence: estimate.** These are nightly rates multiplied by 30 — the worst-case rate a
one-night tourist pays, sourced from Kayak
(https://www.kayak.com/Bellevue-Hotels-Hyatt-House-Bellevue.186902.ksp), not a quote. **This is
the case where "the real quote will come in lower" is not a hedge — it is the entire thesis of
the page.**

* **30 nights, worst case:** ~$121–183/night × 30 = **$3,630 – $5,490**. Over budget by
  $330–$2,190.
* **60 nights, worst case:** **$7,260 – $10,980 total**, or the same $3,630–$5,490/month.
* **What has to happen for this to work:** the sales manager's 30+ night rate has to land at or
  below **$110/night**, which is $3,300/month before the tax exemption. That is roughly a 10%
  cut off the bottom of the rack range — not a heroic ask for a brand that sells long stays as
  a product line.
* **Then apply the tax exemption:** 12–15% off $3,300 is another $400–500/month of effective
  savings, which is what actually creates the margin here.

Add the pet fee if applicable — **$100–200 per stay**, which is a per-stay charge, not per night.
Compare that to Larkspur's $25/night ($750/month). If Tom has a pet, Hyatt House's pricing gets
meaningfully more competitive.

## The Washington 30-day tax rule

Under [WAC 458-20-166](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=458-20-166), a guest becomes
"nontransient" at **30 continuous days** and lodging tax stops applying — worth roughly **12–15%**
on the Eastside, or **$300–450/month**. At this price point that is the high end of that range —
**call it $450–$500/month**, which is the single largest tax saving of any property in the search.

The clock requires **unbroken occupancy**: one reservation, no checkout. Some hotels drop the tax
only from day 30 forward; others refund it **retroactively** across the first 29 days if you
contract 30+ nights up front. That difference is about **$350** — and at this rate, more. Ask
which.

**Never book 29 nights.** All the cost, none of the exemption. The listed minimum here is 1
night, which means the booking system will happily sell you 29 and take the tax. Do not let it.

## When you call

**Call 425-747-2705 and ask for the Sales Manager or Director of Sales. Do not negotiate with
the front desk** — the CSV is explicit about this, and it is the single most important tactical
note on the page. The front desk can only sell you the rate you already saw online. The sales
department is where the extended-stay program lives.

1. **I am looking for your extended-stay program rate. What is the all-in rate for 30 nights, and
   for 60 nights?** (Not the rate on hyatt.com.)
2. Does lodging tax drop at day 30, and is it refunded retroactively on the first 29 days if I
   contract 30+ nights up front?
3. Self-parking is listed as free including covered — confirm. **The pet fee is listed at
   $100–200 per stay — confirm it is per stay, not per night.** How often is housekeeping on an
   extended-stay rate?
4. Is there an elevator? Can you hold a ground-floor unit, or one with grab bars?
5. **The studio kitchen suite is listed as having no oven — what exactly is in it?** Is there a
   cooktop/stovetop, or only a microwave? Does the 1BR have a fuller kitchen, and what does it
   cost?
