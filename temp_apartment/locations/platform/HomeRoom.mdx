## Why this isn't a fit

**Disqualifier #1 — it's roommate coliving.** HomeRoom rents you **a private bedroom inside a house
where other people live.** The charter is not ambiguous: **"No cohabitating and no roommates. Tom gets
his own place."** A locking bedroom door in a shared house is not his own place. He would share the
kitchen, the living room, and often a bathroom, with strangers he did not choose. For a 78-year-old
on a two-month stay, that isn't a compromise on amenities — it's a different product entirely.

**Disqualifier #2 — it isn't here.** HomeRoom has **no Pacific Northwest market**. No Seattle, no
Bellevue, no Redmond. Even if the model were acceptable, there is nothing to book.

Either fact alone ends it. Both together make this the clearest dead end in the search.

## Ruling out the whole coliving category

**This is the real purpose of this page.** HomeRoom is documented not because anyone was seriously
considering it, but because **the entire coliving category will resurface later looking like a budget
win.** These companies operate the same model:

> **PadSplit · Diggz · SpareRoom · Tripalink · Bungalow · Coliving.com**

**Their attractive sub-$1,500 pricing IS the shared living Tom is avoiding.** That's the whole point
to internalize. Coliving isn't cheap because it's efficient or because someone found an arbitrage —
it's cheap **because you're renting one bedroom instead of one apartment.** The low number and the
roommates are the same fact stated two ways. Any time a listing in this price band looks impossibly
good, the first question is: *is this a whole unit, or a room?*

**Where this trap actually bites:** [Furnished Finder](./Furnished_Finder.mdx) is a genuinely strong
option — deep Redmond inventory, 30-day native term, documented units well under budget — but **it
mixes entire units with private rooms in occupied homes.** The same trap, inside a good platform.
Same defense: filter to entire place, and ask every landlord *"will anyone else be living in the
home?"* The same caution applies to Craigslist's `roo` subarea (use `apa`), HotPads' Rooms section,
and Facebook Marketplace's room-heavy listings.

**If a sub-$1,500 Eastside "apartment" turns up later, it is almost always one of two things:** a room
in a shared house, or a scam. Neither is what Tom needs.

## The price math

**None — and that's deliberate.** Running the arithmetic on a coliving room would imply it's on the
table. It isn't, at any price. A $900/month room and a $3,300/month apartment are not competing
offers; only one of them satisfies the charter.

For reference, the cheapest **charter-compliant** options found — private, whole units, 30-day terms:

* **[Landing — Skyline Redmond](./Landing_Skyline_Redmond.mdx)** — $1,767–2,451, Redmond, covered
  parking (showed zero availability).
* **[Furnished Finder](./Furnished_Finder.mdx)** — a documented Redmond 1BR/2BA at **$1,500/mo** with
  utilities, WiFi, and laundry included.

**Both beat most coliving pricing while giving Tom an entire apartment.** That's the argument that
should end this conversation permanently: the coliving discount isn't even real here.
