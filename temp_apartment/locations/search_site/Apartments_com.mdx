## What it is and how it works

The **largest managed-property database** in the search. If a professionally managed building
exists on the Eastside, it is here. That breadth is real and useful — 2,775 Bellevue rentals
is more than any other site returns.

But breadth is the wrong axis for Tom's problem. The constraint is **term length**, and this
site is built around managed buildings — precisely the operators least willing to write a
1–2 month lease. As the [overview](../../overview.mdx) found, **no conventional apartment
building in Redmond, Bellevue, or Kirkland offers true month-to-month.** This database is a
comprehensive index of exactly that inventory.

## How to search it effectively

```
https://www.apartments.com/redmond-wa/short-term/
https://www.apartments.com/bellevue-wa/short-term/
https://www.apartments.com/kirkland-wa/short-term/
```

Filter path in the UI: **Lease Length → Short Term** (it lives under amenities).

Use this site to **build the call list of managed buildings**, then ask each one the term
question directly. Do not use it to answer the term question — it cannot.

## Does the filter actually work?

**Partly — and the way it fails is specific and worth understanding.**

**The Short Term filter is genuine.** It is a real filter, not a keyword hack like
[Zillow's `_att` paths](./Zillow.mdx). That is a point in its favor.

**But the flag is set at the PROPERTY level by the leasing office.** This is the catch. When
a building checks the "short term" box, it is saying:

> *"We sometimes do short terms."*

It is **not** saying:

> *"A month-to-month unit is available right now."*

Those are very different claims, and the filter cannot distinguish them. A building that once
wrote a 6-month lease for someone qualifies for the flag permanently.

**Two more things the filter will not tell you:**

1. **"Short term" here usually means 3–6 months, not 1–2.** Tom needs one to two. Most of
   these results are double or triple that. See the [overview](../../overview.mdx) — the
   shortest conventional terms found anywhere on the Eastside were 2 months.
2. **The premium is $300–800/mo.** This is the number that breaks the budget. A typical
   Eastside 1BR at **~$2,400** becomes **~$3,000–3,300** once the short-term premium applies —
   landing exactly at or above the $3,300 ceiling. The listed price is never the short-term
   price.

Net: a real filter over the wrong inventory, priced at the edge of budget. **Useful as a
directory of buildings to call. Not useful as an answer.**
